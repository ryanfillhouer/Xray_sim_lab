Refinement primer03

A practical guide to what you do after you have a pattern. Workflows, what each refinable parameter does, and how to read the fit quality.

Refinement workflows

Choose a workflow based on what you need to extract. Each method makes different assumptions and refines different parameters.
Single-peak fittingsimplest

Fit individual peaks with a Lorentzian, Gaussian, or pseudo-Voigt profile. No structural model. Extract peak position, integrated intensity, and FWHM per peak.

Use when: you need a single lattice parameter from one well-isolated reflection; characterizing peak shape (Scherrer or Williamson-Hall); tracking one reflection over time/temperature in a multi-phase sample.

Avoid when: peaks overlap heavily, or you need overall structural information.

Le Bailwhole-pattern

Whole-pattern fit using unit cell, space group, and profile shape — but with intensities treated as free, partitioned by Rietveld decomposition. No structural model needed.

Use when: accurate lattice parameters from a complex multi-peak pattern; characterizing profile shape (Caglioti U, V, W); preparing for structure solution; structure is unknown or uncertain.

Avoid when: you need phase quantities or atomic positions — those require Rietveld.

Pawleywhole-pattern

Like Le Bail but treats integrated intensities as independent fitted parameters with uncertainties. Slower, more parameters, but gives intensity error bars directly.

Use when: integrated intensities (with uncertainties) for structure solution; covariance information between intensities.

Avoid when: severe peak overlap makes intensity correlations ill-defined; Le Bail is preferred for unit-cell-only work.

Rietveldstructural

Full structural refinement. Refines atom positions, site occupancies, displacement parameters (B-factors), preferred orientation, scale factor, alongside unit cell, profile, and background.

Use when: you have a structural starting model and need to refine atom positions, occupancies, or quantitative phase fractions; confirming structure solution; characterizing site disorder.

Avoid when: you do not have a structural starting model — use Le Bail first, then solve the structure separately.

Quantitative phase analysismulti-phase

Rietveld on a multi-phase mixture, with each phase scale factor refined. Phase weight fractions from the Hill-Howard equation using ZMV products. Internal standard or PONKCS for amorphous content.

Use when: weight percentages of crystalline phases; process samples, cement, geological materials.

Caveats: microabsorption and preferred orientation can bias results substantially; an internal standard helps for absolute scale.

Whole-powder-pattern modelingmicrostructure

Refines microstructural parameters (size, strain, defects) directly to the pattern using physically-motivated profiles. Outputs grain size distributions, dislocation densities, stacking faults.

Use when: microstructure is the primary interest — cold-worked metals, nanocrystalline materials, defective films.

Avoid when: you only need lattice parameters or quick phase ID — overkill.

Quick decision tree:
  • Just need lattice constant? Single-peak on a high-2θ reflection.
  • Need accurate unit cell from a complex pattern? Le Bail.
  • Need atom positions or phase fractions? Rietveld.
  • Need integrated intensities with errors for structure solution? Pawley.
  • Need grain size, microstrain, defect density? WPPM.

Refinement parameters

Refinement parameters and what they change in the pattern. Most of these can be visualized in real time with the controls on the left.
Background (polynomial / Chebyshev)
Effect: baseline level under all peaks. Slowly varying. Higher-order terms capture curvature from air scatter, fluorescence, or amorphous halo.
Refine first — a wrong background biases all peak intensities. Typically 3-6 terms suffice. Negative background values indicate over-fitting.
Zero shift / sample displacement (s)
Effect: shifts all peaks by Δ(2θ) ≈ −2s cos θ / R for displacement, or by a constant for zero shift. Strongest effect at low 2θ.
A sample sitting 50 µm above the surface gives ~0.05° shift at 20° 2θ. Refine displacement when peaks consistently shift in one direction.
Lattice parameters (a, b, c, α, β, γ)
Effect: sets all peak positions via Bragg's law and the d-spacing formula. Affects every peak.
Refine after background and zero shift are stable. High-symmetry systems (cubic, tetragonal, hexagonal) have built-in constraints. Watch correlation with zero shift.
Scale factor
Effect: overall intensity multiplier. In multi-phase refinement, the ratio of scale factors enters the QPA equation.
Always free. In multi-phase fits, refine each phase's scale factor independently.
Profile FWHM: Caglioti U, V, W
Effect: sets FWHM as FWHM² = U·tan²θ + V·tanθ + W. U captures strain broadening (grows at high 2θ); V is a cross term; W is the instrumental constant.
Calibrate U, V, W on a standard like LaB₆ or NIST 660b SRM first to separate instrument from sample. Free U for samples with microstrain.
Profile shape: η (pseudo-Voigt mixing)
Effect: mixes Gaussian (η=0) and Lorentzian (η=1) character. η typically 0.3-0.7 for routine work. Affects tail shape.
May refine as η = η₀ + X·2θ to capture angular dependence. Important when modeling far peak tails.
Crystallite size L (Scherrer)
Effect: Lorentzian broadening β = K·λ / (L cos θ). Dominates at low 2θ (cos θ ≈ 1).
Below ~5 nm, peaks broaden so much they overlap. Above ~500 nm, broadening is at the instrumental limit. Williamson-Hall separates size from strain.
Microstrain ε
Effect: Gaussian broadening β = 4ε tan θ. Dominates at high 2θ (tan θ → ∞).
Cold-worked metals: 0.1-0.5%. Annealed: under 0.05%. Williamson-Hall plot (β cos θ vs sin θ) separates this from size broadening.
Preferred orientation (March-Dollase r)
Effect: biases relative intensities. r < 1 enhances (hkl) along the preferred axis; r > 1 suppresses it; r = 1 is isotropic.
Common in plate-like (clays, micas) or needle-like (asbestos, some pharmaceuticals) samples. Refining PO can absorb misfit from other parameters — interpret carefully.
Atomic positions (Rietveld only)
Effect: affects structure factors and hence relative peak intensities. Special positions are constrained by site symmetry.
Refine only the free coordinates allowed by the space group Wyckoff positions. Constrain related atoms (e.g. mixed sites in solid solutions) until the fit stabilizes.
Site occupancies (Rietveld only)
Effect: affects effective scattering from each site. Strongly correlated with B-factors.
Cannot refine occupancy and B-factor freely on the same site — they are correlated. Fix one, refine the other, or use chemistry constraints.
Displacement parameters (B, U_iso, U_aniso)
Effect: damps high-2θ intensities via exp(−2B sin²θ/λ²) (B-factor) or exp(−2π²U) (U-factor). Higher B → more damping.
Typical B values: 0.5-2 Å² for inorganics at RT, 3-5 Å² for organics. Anisotropic U_ij captures direction-dependent thermal motion.

Goodness-of-fit statistics

Goodness-of-fit statistics tell you how well the refined model matches the data. None is perfect alone — look at several together and always inspect the difference plot.
R_wp (weighted profile)
R_wp² = Σwᵢ(yᵢ,obs − yᵢ,calc)² / Σwᵢ·yᵢ,obs² , wᵢ = 1/σᵢ²
Most-quoted figure of merit. Weighted by counting statistics — high-intensity points contribute more. Sensitive to the whole pattern including background.
Targets: <5% excellent, <10% good, <15% acceptable, >20% something is wrong. High-background or low-statistics patterns can have low R_wp even with a bad fit — always look at χ² too.
R_p (unweighted profile)
R_p = Σ|yᵢ,obs − yᵢ,calc| / Σ|yᵢ,obs|
Unweighted — every data point counts equally. More forgiving of background mismatch than R_wp.
Targets: similar to R_wp but typically 1-3% lower. R_p alone is rarely used as a target.
R_exp (expected)
R_exp² = (N − P) / Σwᵢ·yᵢ,obs²
The minimum R_wp achievable given the data counting statistics (N data points, P parameters). If R_wp = R_exp, the fit captures everything statistically possible.
Not a target itself — it is the floor against which R_wp is compared.
χ² (chi-squared) / GoF
χ² = (R_wp / R_exp)² , GoF = S = R_wp / R_exp
Direct measure of model quality vs statistical limit. χ² = 1 means the model fits to the data's noise — the ideal. χ² > 1 means model misfit dominates; χ² < 1 usually indicates the counting standard deviation was over-estimated.
Targets: χ² < 2 excellent, < 4 good for routine work, > 10 indicates structural or profile model problems. χ² depends strongly on counting time — long counts give tiny σᵢ, making χ² difficult to push to 1.
R_Bragg (R_B)
R_B = Σ|I_obs − I_calc| / Σ|I_obs| (integrated intensities)
Fit quality at the level of integrated peak intensities. Independent of background and profile shape — purely measures whether structure factors are right.
Targets: <5% excellent for structural fits, <10% good. Useful for Rietveld where you want structural fit alone, separate from profile/background issues.
DW (Durbin-Watson) statistic
DW = Σ(rᵢ − rᵢ₋₁)² / Σrᵢ² , rᵢ = yᵢ,obs − yᵢ,calc
Detects serial correlation in residuals. DW = 2 means residuals are random; DW < 1 indicates positive serial correlation (model is missing a feature).
Targets: >1.5 acceptable, >1.9 very good. Low DW often correlates with bad background or missed peaks.
Important caveats:
  • R-factors alone never tell the whole story. Always inspect the difference plot (y_obs − y_calc). A flat difference is the goal; structure in the difference indicates missing features.
  • Long counting times push R_exp down (small σᵢ), so χ² grows even if the fit is qualitatively excellent. Compare χ² only between datasets with similar counting statistics.
  • R-factor improvement during refinement is not the same as model correctness. Adding parameters always reduces R-factors but can cause over-fitting. Watch for unreasonable parameter values, large correlations, and large standard errors.
  • High symmetry, simple structures should reach R_wp < 5%. Complex (organic, modulated, disordered) structures realistically reach 8-15%.
01 / read peak positions
Position is geometry
2θ depends only on d-spacing via Bragg: d = λ / (2 sin θ). d-spacings come from the metric (a, b, c, α, β, γ). A cubic 220 must sit at exactly d = a/√8.
02 / count missing peaks
Absences are centering
Body-centered (I) kills h+k+l = odd. Face-centered (F) demands h, k, l all even or all odd. The ABSENT peaks are the diagnostic, not the present ones.
03 / watch peak splitting
Splitting is symmetry loss
A single cubic peak splits into 2 in tetragonal (a ≠ c) and 3 in orthorhombic. Doublet → tetragonal. Triplet → orthorhombic. Lower symmetry, more lines.
04 / decode the symbol
Read letter by letter
First letter = Bravais (P, I, F, A, B, C, R). Following symbols = symmetry along principal directions. m = mirror, n/a/b/c/d = glide, 2₁ 3₁ 4₂ 6₃ = screw axes.

Symbol decoder03

01 / what's a space group
Lattice plus operations
A space group is the full set of symmetry operations that map the crystal onto itself. Bravais lattice (translations) plus point-group symmetry (rotations, mirrors) plus their combinations (screws, glides). 230 in total.
02 / read the symbol
Position matters
In Pnma, "n" sits at position 1, "m" at 2, "a" at 3. Each position refers to a specific direction in the cell that depends on the crystal system. Watch the decoder map each token to its direction.
03 / toggle operators
See what each does
Click an operator type in the panel to show or hide it in the cell. Watch how a screw axis differs from a pure rotation, and how a glide differs from a mirror, by following one atom under each operation.
04 / orbit the cell
Drag to rotate
Drag inside the cell to rotate. Scroll to zoom. Use [100], [010], [001] camera buttons to view down each axis to recognize how symmetry elements project. Many become parallel pairs of lines from one viewing direction.

Scope & caveats

This tool is a pedagogical visualizer, not a refinement engine. Peak positions, fringe spacing, critical angles, Bragg geometry, space-group symmetry operations, systematic absences, and the qualitative response of every parameter are physically correct. However the intensity calculations use simplified models throughout: kinematical scattering rather than full dynamical theory; representative absorption coefficients (μ ≈ 200 cm⁻¹) rather than material-specific values; isotropic elastic constants rather than X-ray elastic constants by (hkl); Lorentzian + Gaussian convolution rather than full pseudo-Voigt instrument profiles; and Vegard's-law lattice parameters for alloys. Do not use this tool to extract quantitative values from real data. Use it to build intuition about how parameters move features around, predict what a measurement will look like before you take it, and prepare for instrument training.

Specific approximations to be aware of: GIXRD uses a single μ for all materials. The sin²ψ stress page treats Ti, SiO₂, and Si₃N₄ as quasi-cubic for visualization. Thermal expansion uses a single α per material applied uniformly to a/b/c. The space-group viewer renders general-position operations and Wyckoff atoms but does not render bonds, polyhedra, or oxidation-state-specific colorings. The XRR Parratt calculation is single-layer; multilayer stacks are not supported.
